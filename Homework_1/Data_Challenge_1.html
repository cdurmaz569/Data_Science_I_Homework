Background

The United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) has collected statistics on the U.S. dairy condition from the past 15 months and annual statistics from the past 3 years. From this data, consumption of dairy products has changed in both amount and choice. These changes are apparent when examining dairy production, sales, and consumption over both the course of time and location.

Time Series: Production vs Sales of Milk from 1980 to 2014

# get total milk production (lbs)
milk.production <- milkcow.facts[,c(1,4)]

# get total milk product sales (lbs)
milk.sales <- fluid.milk.sales %>% 
  filter(milk_type == "Total Production") %>% select(year, pounds)

# combine data into one dataframe for visualization 
milk.processing.years <- milk.production %>% 
  inner_join(milk.sales, by = "year") 

# rename columns of dataframe and melt to input to ggplot
names(milk.processing.years) <- c("Year", "Produced", "Sales")

# get ratio of average of milk produced:sold
ratio.milk.process <- round(mean(milk.processing.years$Produced)/
  mean(milk.processing.years$Sales))

# melt data so each row is 'year-production/sale-pounds'
milk.processing.years <- melt(milk.processing.years, id.vars = "Year")

# time course line plot of milk processing over the years
ggplot(milk.processing.years, aes(x = Year)) + 
  geom_line(aes(y = value, col = variable), size = 1.5) + # line plot
  scale_color_manual(labels = c("Produced", "Sales"), # change color of lines
                     values = c("Produced" = "firebrick1", 
                                "Sales" = "springgreen2")) + 
  labs(title = "Pounds of Milk Produced and Sold (1980-2014)", # add title
       x = "Year (1980 - 2014)", # change x axis label
       y = "Pounds of Milk per Year (lb/year)",
       caption = "Figure 1") # change y axis label

Figure 1. This plot shows the total milk production (‘Produced’, red) and milk product sold (‘Sales’, green) in pounds annually from 1980 to 2014. Compared to sales, an average of around 3 times more pounds of milk seem to be produced. While milk production has steadily increased over the 34 years, milk sales remain fairly unchanged from 1980 to 2010, before beginning to slightly drop after 2010.

Figure 2. This grouped boxplot displays the average pounds of milk produced across regions of the US every 17 years from 1980 to 2014. In terms of milk produced over time, of the ten regions five showed clear increases (Lake States, Mountain, Northeast, Pacific, and Southern Plains), two showed clear decreases (Appalachian, Delta States), and three showed slight to no discernible increase (Corn Belt, Northern Plains, Southeast). Interestingly, the regions showing a clear increase produce more pounds of milk on average.

Figure 5. These scatter plots show the correlation between various factors milk cow owning farmers have considered over three decades. (A) Over time, there has been a negative correlation with the increase in cost of cows and the drop in the average number of cows owned. (B) Despite the clear drop in cow ownership, there is a clear positive correlation between the average pounds of milk produced per cow and the total milk produced as time progresses. (C) As the average milk produced per cow could be a potential confounding variable in the relationship between cow cost and cow ownership shown in A, the average milk produced per cow was compared to the average number of cows and showed an almost identical negative correlation to A. (D) Interestingly, this information helps make sense of the seemingly contradicting negative correlation of cow ownership and total milk produced, because if the cows are producing more milk on average, farmers can own less cows.
